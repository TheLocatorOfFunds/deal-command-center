# How surplus recovery works

A plain-English guide to what happens when a foreclosure sells your house for more than what was owed, and how RefundLocators helps you recover the difference.

## The short version

When a property is sold at sheriff's sale, the bank gets paid first. Anything left over is called the **surplus**. That money belongs to you — the former owner — but the court doesn't mail it to you. You have to file a claim to receive it. Most people don't know they can claim it, so it sits at the courthouse for years until the state eventually takes it.

RefundLocators files the claim on your behalf, attends the hearings, and gets the funds released to you. We work on contingency — if we don't recover anything, you don't pay anything.

## Q: How much money am I owed?

The amount depends on what your house sold for at auction versus what was owed on the mortgage. We look at the public court file, do the math, and tell you the estimated range before you sign anything.

For most cases we work, the surplus is between $20,000 and $200,000. We don't take cases under about $5,000 because the legal cost makes it not worth your time or ours.

## Q: Why didn't the court just send me the money?

Ohio law requires the former owner to formally claim surplus funds. The court won't disburse the money until someone files a written motion, attends a hearing, and proves identity and right to the funds. The process takes 90 to 180 days and involves specific paperwork the court accepts.

Most people who lose a home don't know they're entitled to anything, and the court has no obligation to notify them. After 5 years, unclaimed surplus is forfeited to the state.

## Q: Will I have to go to court?

Almost never. We have an attorney who appears on your behalf. You may have to sign a notarized affidavit confirming your identity and that you're entitled to the funds. We'll walk you through that part.

## Q: How long does it take?

Typical Ohio surplus case: 90 to 180 days from the day we file your claim. Some cases close faster, some take longer if probate is involved (when the original homeowner has passed away and heirs need to be sorted out by the court).

You'll get updates from us at every step — engagement signed, claim filed, hearing scheduled, distribution order entered, funds received. If something stalls, we'll tell you why.

## Q: Can I do this myself?

Yes. You can file the claim, find a probate attorney if needed, attend the hearings, and handle the paperwork yourself. It usually takes 6 to 12 months and a few hundred dollars in court fees. Some people prefer that path. We don't talk anyone out of it.

If you'd rather have someone else handle the whole thing for a percentage of the recovery, that's what we do.

## Q: What information do you need from me to start?

To file your claim we need:

- A signed engagement agreement (we send it via DocuSign — takes 30 seconds)
- A copy of your photo ID
- Proof of address (a recent utility bill or lease usually works)
- Any letters or paperwork you received about the foreclosure

If your former spouse or another family member is also entitled to the funds, we'll need to talk through that — multi-claimant cases need a few extra steps.
